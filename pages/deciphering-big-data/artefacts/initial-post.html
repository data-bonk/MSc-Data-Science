Initial Post

I initially produced an initial post based on the incorrect link attached to the Collaborative Discussion 1 moodle page. I rewrote my initial post based on the correct reference below.

The Internet of Things (IoT) enables vast, continuous data collection across connected devices and offers real-time insights into environments, infrastructure, and human behaviour. While the rationale lies in improving decision-making through automation and analytics (Ashton, 2009), IoT also introduces major data quality challenges. Huxley et al. (2020) emphasise that data cleaning is not just for preparing data but is fundamental in producing valid, ethical insights. This is especially relevant to IoT, where data is generated at scale, often in unstructured or inconsistent formats. Traditional cleaning approaches are not well-suited to this high-velocity context, making it difficult to ensure accuracy and trustworthiness in real-time. Although IoT enabled opportunities in predictive maintenance, smart cities, and resource optimisation (Atzoria et al., 2010), its limitations are rooted in the complexity of integrating, validating, and cleaning vast heterogeneous datasets. On top of that, risks escalate with scale: each connected device widens the attack surface, raising significant security and privacy concerns (Roman et al., 2013). In line with Huxley et al. (2020), effective IoT deployment must embed data quality practices at its core. Without robust, scalable cleaning and governance mechanisms, the value and ethical legitimacy of IoT insights remain in question.

References
