Unit 8 Initial Post

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) Article 32 requires organisations to implement “appropriate technical and organisational measures” to ensure the security of personal data, tailored to the level of risk involved (European Commission, 2016). This risk-based approach demands consideration of the “state of the art,” implementation costs, and the nature of the data, promoting flexibility and proportionality in securing information (Voigt and Von dem Bussche, 2017).

The UK Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO) adopts these GDPR principles but provides more detailed, prescriptive guidance through its ‘security’ principle (ICO, 2023). The ICO emphasises practical steps such as encryption, access controls, and regular security assessments, particularly targeting sectors like public services where accidental data leaks have been prevalent (ICO, 2022).

Regarding exemptions, the GDPR allows derogations for scientific research, provided safeguards such as pseudonymisation and data minimisation are implemented (Peloquin et al., 2020). The ICO also provides guidance on pseudonymisation techniques to strike a balance between data utility and privacy during research activities (ICO, 2023). Both frameworks, however, face challenges adapting to emerging technologies like machine learning, where automated processing complicates risk assessments (McKinney, 2022).

In summary, GDPR’s flexible, principle-based framework contrasts with the ICO’s sector-specific recommendations. Organisations must navigate this balance to ensure compliance while fostering innovation in data processing.
