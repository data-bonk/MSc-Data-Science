Summary Post

For my collaborative discussion summary post, I looked at Mariam and Anna Matilda’s initial posts, and used those to improve and compare my initial research.

The Internet of Things (IoT) represents a transformative leap in real-time data acquisition and decision-making. In my initial post, I highlighted how IoT’s potential is underpinned by vast, continuous data streams, which can drive predictive maintenance, smart city development, and resource efficiency (Atzori et al., 2010). However, this vision relies on scalable and ethical data cleaning practices, as highlighted by Huxley et al. (2020). The limitations and risks, including inconsistent data formats, security vulnerabilities, and ethical concerns, must be tackled for IoT to deliver meaningful value. Mariam’s post reinforces these points, particularly the centrality of data cleaning in ensuring valid insights from IoT. Her reference to the dangers of Type I and Type II errors (Osborne, 2013) adds nuance to the risk of misinformed automation and alerts. She further broadens the discussion by emphasising interoperability and infrastructure challenges (Ray, 2018), expanding the scope of limitations. Anna Matilda complements this with her emphasis on cloud-based architectures such as Azure IoT Hub, which offer scalable solutions, but also necessitate robust governance. Her inclusion of ethical concerns, particularly those related to surveillance and data consent (Zuboff, 2019), adds a crucial socio-political dimension. Furthermore, her reference to Kandel et al. (2011) supports the shared view that unstructured IoT data requires substantial processing for trustworthy insights. Overall, our posts converge on the importance of embedding data governance, security, and quality mechanisms into IoT systems from inception. As Huxley et al. (2020) argue, cleaning is not a preparatory step but a foundational practice in big data analytics. Without such precision, the promises of IoT risk being overshadowed by compromised accuracy and ethical pitfalls.
